Lesson 2: The First Declension and Conjugation


1. The First Declension

Before beginning, take a look at the endings of the first declension at grammar number 23. It might not make any sense at first, so a few noun examples might help. The first declension nouns are nouns that use the endings shown in the graph of grammar number 23. The explanation on how the endings work will be given shortly. The gender is mostly feminine but there are a few nouns that have a masculine English meaning which means that the noun has to be masculine (you can find this rule at grammar number 24). Consider the vocabulary below:

The blue text represents that the word is a noun in the voabulary.

Most of our English language originated from Latin. If you compared the endings with the nouns in the vocabulary, then you will see that each noun stem first starts with the nominative personal ending which is then followed by the stem with the genitive personal ending. The stem is what defines the noun’s meaning while the ending defines the noun’s case. Consider the word terra. The stem is “terr” and the nominative ending is -a. Note: Latin has no articles (a, an, or the), so when you translate from Latin to English, you have to add them back in wherever the translation sounds odd (“terra” means “the land” as a subject).

The cases are pretty simple to figure out. Singular can be abbreviated as “S.” and plural can be abbreviated as “P.” Consider the following declining of terra:

The First Declension
A. Nominative Case: (S.) “terr-a” means “land” or “the land,” and (P.) “terr-ae” means “lands” or “the lands.”
B. Genitive Case: (S.) “terr-ae” means “of the land” or “the land’s,” and (P.) “terr-ārum” means “of the lands” or “the lands’.”
C. Dative Case: (S.) “terr-ae” means “to or for the land,” and (P.) “terr-īs” means “to or for the lands.”
D. Accusative Case: (S.) “terr-am” means “(direct object) the land,” and (P.) “terr-ās” means “(direct object) the lands.”
E. Ablative Case: (S.) “terr-ā” means “(preposition) the land,” and (P.) “terr-īs” means “(preposition) the lands.”
